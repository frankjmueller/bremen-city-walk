  2. 1

    Stop 1 · Marktplatz

    St Peter's Cathedral

    1 min from Domsheide — walk towards the twin green spires.

    Bremen's cathedral has watched over the city for more than 1,200 years. Its strangest secret lies underneath: the Bleikeller (Lead Cellar), where eight mummies rest in open coffins — craftsmen, soldiers, even a countess. Nobody embalmed them; the cellar's dry air simply refused to let them decay, and no one could explain it for centuries.

    Dare to look? The Lead Cellar is open daily in summer (Saturdays only until early afternoon) for a small fee. Entrance beside the cathedral.

  3. 2

    Stop 2 · Town hall, west side

    The Town Musicians

    2 min — cross the square, around the town hall's left corner.

    Donkey, dog, cat, rooster: four ageing animals who ran away rather than be discarded, scared off a band of robbers with the loudest concert of their lives — and never actually reached Bremen. The city adopted them anyway, and this bronze from 1951 became its most-loved landmark.

    The ritual: hold both of the donkey's front legs with both hands and make a wish. One hand only? Locals will smile — that's just one donkey greeting another. You'll see why the legs shine like gold.

  4. 3

    Stop 3 · Marktplatz · UNESCO World Heritage

    Roland & the Town Hall

    A few steps back onto the market square.

    The stone knight is Roland, standing here since 1404 with his sword drawn and his eyes fixed on the cathedral — the citizens' message to the bishop that this city governs itself. Legend says Bremen stays free as long as Roland stands, so a spare head is said to be kept in storage, just in case. The gap between his knees was once an official unit of measure: the Bremen ell, used by cloth merchants.

    Behind him rises the 600-year-old town hall — together they are a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The wine cellar below has been pouring since 1405 and stocks Germany's oldest drinkable wine, from 1653.

  5. 4

    Stop 4 · In front of the glass parliament

    The Bremen Hole

    30 seconds — look down! It's a drain cover in the pavement.

    Bremen's strangest sight is a hole in the ground. Drop a coin into the metal cover in front of the parliament building and listen: a donkey brays, a dog barks, a cat meows, a rooster crows. The Town Musicians live down there — and they say thank you. It's Germany's loudest charity box: every cent goes to a foundation helping people in need across Bremen.

    Keep small coins ready — every guest gets a private concert.

  6. 5

    Stop 5 · Off the market square

    Böttcherstraße

    2 min — find the golden relief above a narrow brick entrance.

    One hundred metres of pure fantasy. Coffee merchant Ludwig Roselius — the man who invented decaffeinated coffee after his father drank himself to death on the regular kind — poured his fortune into this street in the 1920s: expressionist brick, bronze doors, gold everywhere. Enter under the glowing Lightbringer relief.

    Halfway down, thirty Meissen porcelain bells chime between the gables — every hour from 12:00 to 18:00. Worth timing your visit.

    Sweet tooth? In the little craft courtyard at No. 8, the Bremer Bonbon Manufaktur makes candy by hand — watch them pour and cut the sweets through the workshop window.

  7. 6

    Stop 6 · The old fishermen's quarter

    The Schnoor

    6 min — down Böttcherstraße, left along the river, follow the signs.

    Bremen's oldest quarter, and its most magical. Schnoor means “string”: the doll-sized houses line the lanes like pearls on a string — which is exactly how this guide is laid out. Six hundred years ago rope makers and fishermen lived here; today it's the corner of Bremen everyone falls in love with.

    Squeeze through lanes barely a metre wide, hunt for the smallest house in the city, and let yourselves get lost — it's tiny, you can't really stay lost.

    Candy, part two: at Marterburg 30 the Bonbon Manufaktur runs its show workshop — watch bonbons being made the old way.

  8. 7

    Stop 7 · The Weser riverside

    The Schlachte

    8 min — walk back to the river and turn right along the water.

    For five hundred years this was Bremen's harbour: ships from all over Europe tied up here with wine, wool and spices. When the big ships moved downstream, the Schlachte reinvented itself as the city's sunniest mile — beer gardens, historic ships and the best benches on the Weser.

    Perfect lunch break. Pick any terrace, order something cold, watch the river roll towards the North Sea. Toast like a local: “Prost!”

  9. 8

    Stop 8 · Sögestraße

    The Pigs of Sögestraße

    8 min — up through Obernstraße, then into Sögestraße.

    Meet Bremen's most patient residents. Sögestraße literally means “Street of the Sows”: in the Middle Ages, pigs were driven along this very route every morning to the pastures outside the gates — and home again at night. Since 1974 a bronze swineherd and his little herd have kept the tradition alive. Children ride them, everyone pats them, and their backs shine like gold.

    Coffee stop: keep an eye out for a Müller & Egerer bakery — a beloved local chain. Grab a coffee and a pastry the Bremen way.

  10. 9

    Stop 9 · Wallanlagen park

    The Windmill on the Wall

    4 min — Sögestraße ends at the park; the mill is to your left.

    A working-size windmill in the middle of the city! You're standing on Bremen's old star-shaped ramparts. When the walls lost their military purpose 200 years ago, the city didn't build them over — it turned them into one of Germany's first public parks, a green ribbon of gardens and water. A mill has stood on this bastion since the 1600s; this one kept grinding grain into the twentieth century.

    Breather: grab a bench by the moat and enjoy the flower beds — the last stretch of the walk starts here.

  11. 10

    Stop 10 · Nelson-Mandela-Park

    The Elephant — a Namibian story in Bremen

    12 min — through the park, past the station, into Nelson-Mandela-Park.

    This ten-metre brick elephant carries the heaviest story on this walk — and it leads directly to Namibia. Built in 1932, it glorified Germany's colonial empire, including what was then called German South West Africa. Between 1904 and 1908, German troops committed genocide against the Ovaherero and Nama peoples; many of the victims were driven into the Omaheke desert.

    Bremen's citizens changed the monument's meaning: in 1990, days after Namibia's independence, it was rededicated as Germany's first anti-colonial monument. Behind it lies a memorial made of stones brought from the Omaheke itself, honouring the victims. Bremen and Windhoek are partner cities today.

    The memorial of Omaheke stones is in the small hollow directly behind the elephant. Take a quiet moment — this place belongs to your story too.
